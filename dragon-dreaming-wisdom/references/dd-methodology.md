# Dragon Dreaming Methodology — Deep Reference

## Source: John Croft, Dragon Dreaming International
## Open-source methodology, used in 56+ countries since the 1990s

---

## Core Philosophy

Dragon Dreaming is a project design methodology rooted in Aboriginal Australian wisdom and systems thinking. Created by John Croft and Vivienne Elanta in Australia in the 1990s, drawing particularly from the knowledge of the Mardu and Nyungar peoples of Western Australia.

**"Dragon"** = fears, obstacles, insecurities. They are TEACHERS, not enemies. Every dragon you face carries a gift — if you're willing to listen.

**"Dreaming"** = from the Aboriginal concept: "If you've lost your dream, you've lost your soul." Dreams are not fantasies — they are the seeds of all meaningful projects.

**Win-Win-Win**: Every project must serve three levels:
1. **Personal growth** of the people involved
2. **Community benefit** — the group, team, or local community
3. **Service to the Earth** — the environment, humanity, the wider ecosystem

If a project only serves profit or only one person's agenda, it is not Dragon Dreaming.

## The 4 Phases

### DREAMING (25% of project energy)
**Purpose**: Imagine. Explore. No judging. No planning yet.
**Behavior**: LISTEN. Don't execute. Don't evaluate. Collect dreams.
**When done**: When everyone passes consecutively (no more dreams to add).

**Key tool — Dream Circle**:
1. Sit in a circle (or open a conversation). One person speaks at a time.
2. Generative Question: "What would this project have to be like for you to say it was the best use of your time?"
3. Only the current speaker shares. Everyone else practices Pinakarri.
4. All dreams are welcome, even contradictory ones. No filtering.
5. Record every dream — written down or noted.
6. When done, read all dreams back as a completed story (past tense): "We built... We achieved... People felt..."

**Key tool — Pinakarri** (deep listening):
- A practice drawn from Aboriginal tradition (literally "ears standing up" in Mardu/Mandjilidjara language), synthesized with mindfulness practices from Buddhist meditation and Joanna Macy's "The Work that Reconnects"
- Listen without planning your response
- Listen with empathy, not judgment
- Can be called at any moment: say "Pinakarri" and everyone stops and is silent
- Useful when conversation gets heated, scattered, or too fast

**90% of ideas get blocked at the dreaming stage in normal processes. Dragon Dreaming prevents this by protecting the space for wild, unfiltered ideas.**

### PLANNING (25% of project energy)
**Purpose**: Turn dreams into an actionable plan. Be realistic about what's possible.
**Behavior**: Structure. Organize. Map dependencies. Test commitment.

**Key tool — Karabirrdt** (Spider Web):
1. Write the project goal in the center
2. Identify major milestones/nodes around it
3. For each node, break into sub-tasks
4. Draw Song Lines (connections/dependencies between nodes — what must happen before what)
5. Assign each task to a person (or leave open until someone commits)
6. Budget: use collective intuition — everyone writes an estimate, then average
7. Timeline: map when each node starts and ends
8. Test commitment: "Can you give 100% to this task?" — only assign if YES

**Song Lines** = the paths along which information, resources, people, money, and decisions flow between tasks. Named after Aboriginal songlines — the paths that connect places across the land, carrying stories and knowledge. In a Karabirrdt, they show the living connections between nodes — not just sequential dependencies, but channels of energy and communication.

**100% Identification Rule**: Nobody works on a task they don't fully identify with. "Identification" is deeper than commitment — it means you see the task as part of who you are, not just something you promised to do. If not identified, the task gets reassigned or redesigned until someone owns it fully. Half-hearted ownership leads to failure.

**Consensus**: All decisions in Dragon Dreaming are made by consensus. This means everyone's voice matters equally — not majority vote, not top-down decree. Consensus requires Pinakarri (deep listening) and patience. It is slower but produces decisions everyone truly supports.

### DOING (25% of project energy)
**Purpose**: Execute. But with flexibility, not rigidity.
**Behavior**: Work on tasks. Check in regularly. Adapt when needed.

**Key principles**:
- **Flexibility > rigid plan.** Plans change — that's OK. The plan serves the dream, not the other way around.
- **Regular check-ins**: "Are we still aligned with the dream?"
- **If stuck** -> go back to Planning or even Dreaming
- **Supervising questions**: "Is this task still serving the dream?"
- **Democratic supervision** — anyone can raise concerns about direction

**When to go back**:
- New information changes the plan -> back to Planning
- The dream itself evolves -> back to Dreaming
- Something isn't working -> celebrate the learning, then replan

### CELEBRATING (25% of project energy)
**Purpose**: Acknowledge. Learn. Integrate. Thank.
**Behavior**: STOP and celebrate. Even small wins. Even failures.

**Why 25%?**: Most projects skip celebration entirely. DD mandates it because:
- Without celebration, people burn out
- Without reflection, mistakes repeat
- Without gratitude, teams fall apart
- Without pausing, the next dream can't emerge

**What to celebrate**:
- Milestones completed
- Challenges overcome
- Lessons learned (even from failures)
- People's contributions
- The dream evolving into something new

**Celebration process**:
1. Gratitude circle — what are you grateful for?
2. What did we learn?
3. What would we do differently?
4. What's the next dream emerging?
5. Update the project documentation with these lessons

**Celebrating failure**: A "failed" task that teaches something is a SUCCESS in Dragon Dreaming. The learning is more valuable than sticking to a plan that doesn't work.

## The Project Wheel (Circular, Not Linear)

```
        DREAMING
       /         \
CELEBRATING    PLANNING
       \         /
         DOING
```

**Fractal nature**: Each phase contains its own mini-cycle:
- Dreaming about Dreaming, Planning the Dreaming, Doing the Dreaming, Celebrating the Dreaming
- This creates 4 x 4 = 16 sub-steps (simplified to 12 in practice)

**Movement is circular**: After Celebrating, a new Dreaming begins. Projects evolve.
**Movement can go backward**: Stuck in Doing? Back to Planning. Plan doesn't fit? Back to Dreaming. This is healthy, not failure.

## Key Differences from Other Methods

| Aspect | Dragon Dreaming | Agile/Scrum | Waterfall | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Starting point | Dream/vision | User stories | Requirements | Empathy |
| Celebration | 25% mandatory | Sprint retro | Post-project | Reflection |
| Personal growth | Core goal | Not addressed | Not addressed | Not addressed |
| Flexibility | Circular, can go back | Sprint-based | Linear | Iterative |
| Decision making | Consensus | Team vote | Top-down | Team-based |
| Success metric | Win-win-win | Velocity | On-time/budget | User satisfaction |
| Obstacles | Teachers (dragons) | Impediments | Risks | Constraints |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Skipping celebration** — MOST COMMON. DD says 25% of energy. Don't skip it.
2. **Not enough dreaming** — Rushing to plan kills creativity. 90% of ideas die in typical processes.
3. **Premature planning** — "Let's just start" kills the dream. Dream first.
4. **Over-planning** — The plan is a guide, not a prison. Stay flexible.
5. **Skipping Pinakarri** — Without deep listening, real consensus is impossible.
6. **Not testing commitment** — Tasks assigned without 100% identification lead to failure.
7. **Ignoring win-win-win** — If the project only serves profit, it's not DD.
8. **Maintaining hierarchy** — DD is flat. Everyone's dream matters equally.
9. **Treating dragons as enemies** — They are teachers. Ask: "What can we learn from this obstacle?"
10. **Forgetting to return to dreaming** — Projects evolve. The dream evolves too. Circle back.
